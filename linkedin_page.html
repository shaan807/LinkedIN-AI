    Here at LinkedIn, we know that our members are busy, so we want to make it a little bit easier for you to stay on top of some of the legal changes happening on our platform by sharing with you that our user agreement will be updated and effective on November 20th, 2024. Some of the changes being made are to account for updates to new products and features in the generative AI space, as well as license terms that allow our creators to expand their brands even beyond LinkedIn. You'll also see that our privacy policy has been clarified to include information around how we use personal data for our affiliates and ourselves to improve both security and our products in the generative AI space and beyond. You'll also see that we have included an opt out that you can use if you want to opt out of your personal data being used for generative AI purposes. I should mention though, that in the European Economic Area as well as in Switzerland, this setting will not be available because for now, we are pausing the use of personal data from those regions in any of our generative AI content creating modeling. And finally, we just want to note that as technology and the world of work continue to change, we are here to keep you updated. And informed about the changes on our platform.

          Let's cut through the LinkedIn spin machine and clarify what this really means for user-generated content (UGC). TL;DR -> If you create content on LinkedIn, you should formally opt out of having your intellectual property used for AI training. When you publish content on LinkedIn, while you retain "ownership" of your IP, the platform’s policy grants broad rights to LinkedIn and Microsoft to use it for Generative AI purposes. This means: - LinkedIn can and will create derivative works from your posts, articles, or comments. - You don’t own or control those derivative works and you won’t receive compensation if they are used commercially. This is a royalty-free license granted to LinkedIn and Microsoft that allows them to commercially benefit from UGC, while YOU, the original creator, see no financial return for the value your IP generates. NOTE: Even if you opt-out, it's only a going forward opt-out. LinkedIn and Microsoft retain the rights to use all prior UGC to generate and monetize derivative works.

                I recently highlighted (though was certainly not the first to spot) that LinkedIn had been using members’ personal data in its generative AI (GenAI) systems before updating its User Agreement or making changes to its Privacy Policy. Clearly, this was something that bothered a lot of members. LinkedIn has now made changes to the above documents to clarify that members have the ability to opt out of having their data used for GenAI purposes. I would have thought that having people opt IN would have been the right thing to do. Do you think LinkedIn’s concern was that no one would bother to go into their settings to toggle the data use permission to “on”? Note that in the EU and Switzerland, the opt-out option does not apply because, for now at least, LinkedIn is not using any personal data belonging to members with IP addresses in those regions. I can appreciate there may be benefits to members when GenAI learns from their data to improve the platform for everyone. But at the end of the day, it’s still personal data. What do you think? Has LinkedIn taken the right path on AI governance here? #AI #GenAI #AIGovernance #personaldata #privacy #EU
